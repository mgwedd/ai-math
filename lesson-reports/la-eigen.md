# la-eigen — Eigenvectors & Eigenvalues

## Current summary (what it teaches + what the lab does)
The lesson introduces the defining equation **M·v = λ·v** and the intuition that eigenvectors are the "special directions a matrix can't rotate — only stretch." It uses the concrete symmetric example `M = [[2,1],[1,2]]`, correctly stating its two eigendirections: `[1,1]` with λ=3 and `[1,−1]` with λ=1, and notes every other vector gets pulled toward the dominant eigendirection. The `ml` note connects eigenvectors to PCA (eigenvectors of the covariance matrix = directions of max variance), Google PageRank, and Hessian eigenvalues for diagnosing valleys vs. saddle points. Three quiz questions cover: (1) λ=3 means same direction ×3 length; (2) the diagonal of a diagonal matrix gives the eigenvalues; (3) PCA projects onto the top eigenvectors of the covariance matrix. The `deeper` cards give per-wrong-option explanations, and notably introduce trace = sum of eigenvalues and det = product of eigenvalues as distractor-debunkers.

**The lab (`INTERACTIVES.eigen`)**: a single 2D plane where the learner drags a purple vector **v**; a second arrow shows **M·v** (red while M rotates v, green when they align within ~2.2°). A live readout shows the angle(v, Mv) and the stretch ratio ‖Mv‖/‖v‖. Two faint dashed gold lines hint at the eigendirections (the y=x and y=−x diagonals). Three gated missions: (1) find the ×3 eigendirection (parallel AND ratio>2.8); (2) find the λ=1 eigendirection (parallel AND ratio<1.2); (3) break alignment so M rotates v by >15°.

## Strengths
- The math is **correct**: eigenvalues of `[[2,1],[1,2]]` are genuinely 3 and 1, with eigenvectors `[1,1]` and `[1,−1]`. (det=3, trace=4 ✓; char. poly (2−λ)²−1=0 → λ=3,1.)
- The "stretch, never rotate" framing matches the canonical 3Blue1Brown intuition exactly.
- The lab's drag-to-discover mechanic is pedagogically excellent: the learner *physically searches* for the invariant directions and feels the "pull toward the dominant eigendirection," which is the single best eigen-intuition available in 2D.
- The `deeper` cards already surface trace (sum) and determinant (product) as eigenvalue invariants — sophisticated and exactly the right level for a master's-bound learner.
- The ML note's three applications (PCA, PageRank, Hessian) are all real and well-chosen.

## Inaccuracies / fidelity issues (each: the issue -> the correct statement -> source URL)
1. **Over-generalization: "special vectors only get stretched, never rotated" stated as if every matrix has them.** Many real matrices have **no real eigenvectors at all** (e.g. a 90° rotation `[[0,−1],[1,0]]` rotates *every* vector — its eigenvalues are ±i, purely complex), and a shear `[[1,1],[0,1]]` has only a *single* line of eigenvectors (it is *defective* — not enough eigenvectors to form a basis). The lesson never flags that existence of real eigenvectors is conditional. -> Correct: "Some matrices have eigendirections; some (rotations) have none in the reals; some (shears) have a deficient set. Whether real eigenvectors exist is itself informative." -> https://www.3blue1brown.com/lessons/eigenvalues/ and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defective_matrix

2. **The symmetric example is presented as if it were a generic matrix, hiding a special structure.** `[[2,1],[1,2]]` is **symmetric**, so by the **spectral theorem** its eigenvectors are guaranteed real and *orthogonal* (`[1,1]⊥[1,−1]`). That orthogonality is exactly *why* PCA works (covariance matrices are symmetric). The lesson uses a symmetric matrix to motivate PCA but never states the property that makes the bridge valid — a missed teaching opportunity and a subtle fidelity gap (a generic non-symmetric matrix's eigenvectors are *not* orthogonal). -> Correct: "Symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors (spectral theorem); this is precisely why PCA's principal axes are perpendicular." -> https://web.stanford.edu/class/cs168/l/l8.pdf

3. **"Every other vector gets rotated toward the dominant eigendirection" is true only for repeated application (power iteration), not a single multiply.** A single `M·v` for this matrix tilts v toward `[1,1]`, but the strong "converges to the dominant eigendirection" claim is the behavior of M^k v as k→∞ (power method), not one application. As literally worded it slightly overstates one-shot behavior. -> Correct: "Repeatedly applying M drives almost any vector toward the top-eigenvalue direction — this is power iteration, the engine behind PageRank." -> https://www.cs.columbia.edu/~djhsu/AML/lectures/notes-pca.pdf

4. **PageRank claim is true but unqualified.** PageRank is the dominant (Perron) eigenvector (λ=1) of the column-stochastic Google matrix, found by power iteration — worth saying it's the λ=1 eigenvector, not just "an eigenvector," to connect it to mission 2's λ=1 case. -> https://www.3blue1brown.com/lessons/eigenvalues/

5. **Quiz Q2 framing risk (not an error, a subtlety):** "the diagonal entries ARE the eigenvalues" is true *only because the matrix is diagonal* (or, more generally, triangular). Stated without the qualifier a learner may overgeneralize to "diagonal entries of any matrix are its eigenvalues," which is false. The WRONG_WHY cards are good but the correct-answer `why` should pin the qualifier. -> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues_and_eigenvectors

## Conceptual gaps (what a serious learner still needs)
- **The characteristic polynomial det(M − λI) = 0.** The lesson gives no *method* to find eigenvalues — only asserts them. A master's-bound learner must see *why* (M−λI) being singular (det=0) is the condition, tying this lesson back to the determinant lesson. This is the single biggest gap.
- **Existence/non-existence**: rotations → complex eigenvalues (no real eigenvectors); shears → defective (repeated eigenvalue, one eigenvector line). The current lesson implies eigenvectors always exist.
- **Diagonalization / eigenbasis**: M = PDP⁻¹, and why this makes M^k trivial (D^k = diag of λ^k). This is the payoff that powers PCA whitening, matrix exponentials, recurrent dynamics, and PageRank's convergence rate (set by λ₂/λ₁).
- **Spectral theorem** for symmetric matrices (real, orthogonal eigenvectors) — the missing rigorous link to PCA.
- **Eigenvalue magnitude = stability/growth rate**: |λ|>1 grows, |λ|<1 decays under repeated application; sign of λ flips direction; λ=0 ↔ det=0 ↔ non-invertible. Connects to RNN exploding/vanishing gradients and to the Hessian (curvature sign = convex/concave/saddle).
- **Trace = Σλ, det = Πλ** as invariants — already in `deeper` cards but deserves promotion into the main `learn` text since it gives a free sanity check.

## Lab ideas (concrete: name the interactive, what the learner manipulates, what it reveals)
1. **Upgrade the existing `eigen` lab with a matrix selector / sliders.** Currently M is hard-coded. Add a small set of preset buttons or 2–4 sliders for M's entries with live readout of the *computed* eigenvalues/eigenvectors. Add presets: **Symmetric** `[[2,1],[1,2]]` (orthogonal eigendirs), **Rotation** `[[0,−1],[1,0]]` (the green-align mission becomes *impossible* — Mv can never be parallel to v, revealing "no real eigenvectors / complex λ"), **Shear** `[[1,1],[0,1]]` (only one eigendirection along the x-axis — learner discovers the deficiency), and **Reflection** `[[1,0],[0,−1]]` (negative eigenvalue along y). This single change teaches existence, complex eigenvalues, and defectiveness experientially. Add a fourth mission: "Pick the rotation preset and try mission 1 — discover that *no* drag aligns Mv with v. That's a complex eigenvalue."
2. **"Power iteration" mode (new interactive `eigen-power`).** A button "Apply M again" repeatedly transforms v, drawing a fading trail of M·v, M²·v, M³·v normalized. The learner watches almost any start vector converge to the `[1,1]` direction; a second readout shows the angle to `[1,1]` shrinking each step. Reveals: dominant eigenvector, convergence rate ≈ (λ₂/λ₁)^k, and *exactly* how PageRank is computed. Mission: "Apply M five times from any start and watch v lock onto the dominant eigendirection."
3. **"Eigenbasis = stretch in disguise" (new interactive).** Show the unit circle of vectors transformed into an ellipse by M; highlight that the ellipse's *axes* lie along the eigenvectors and their *half-lengths* are the eigenvalues. Slider over M reveals det = product of axis lengths (area scale), trace = sum. (For symmetric M the ellipse axes are perpendicular — visual spectral theorem.) This is the most illuminating static picture for eigen and directly previews SVD.
4. **PCA mini-lab.** A 2D scatter of correlated points; learner drags a candidate axis and the lab reports projected variance; the maximum is found exactly at the top eigenvector of the (symmetric) covariance matrix, which is then revealed. Closes the loop from the `ml` note to a hands-on artifact.

## Content improvements (specific learn/ml/deeper text upgrades)
- **Add the eigenvalue-finding method to `learn`**: one line — "To *find* eigenvalues, ask when M−λI squishes space flat: solve **det(M − λI) = 0**. For our matrix that's (2−λ)²−1 = 0 → λ = 3 or 1." This wires the lesson to the determinant lesson and removes the "magic numbers" feel.
- **Add an existence caveat to `learn`**: "Not every matrix has real eigendirections — a pure rotation knocks *every* vector off its span (its eigenvalues are complex), and a shear has only one. Whether real eigenvectors exist tells you something deep about the map." (Optionally back this with a rotation preset in the lab.)
- **State the symmetry/spectral fact** where PCA is mentioned: "Our `[[2,1],[1,2]]` is *symmetric*, so its eigenvectors are perpendicular (the spectral theorem). Covariance matrices are always symmetric — that's why PCA's principal directions come out orthogonal."
- **Promote trace/det from `deeper` into `learn`** as a sanity check: "Quick check: eigenvalues sum to the trace (3+1 = 4 = 2+2 ✓) and multiply to the determinant (3×1 = 3 ✓)."
- **`ml` upgrade**: clarify PageRank is the **dominant (λ=1) eigenvector found by power iteration**, and that the **convergence speed is set by λ₂/λ₁**. For the Hessian line, sharpen: "all-positive eigenvalues ⇒ local minimum (convex bowl), all-negative ⇒ maximum, mixed signs ⇒ saddle; in high dimensions almost all critical points are saddles." Add diagonalization payoff: "Writing M = PDP⁻¹ turns M^k into D^k — raising eigenvalues to a power — which is why eigenvalues govern stability (|λ|>1 explodes, |λ|<1 decays), the heart of RNN exploding/vanishing gradients."
- **Fix the power-iteration wording**: change "Every other vector gets rotated toward the dominant eigendirection" → "*Repeatedly applying* M pulls almost any vector toward the dominant (λ=3) direction."

## Quiz improvements (specific misconceptions to target; keep questions self-contained — never require recalling lab-graph data)
Keep the three current questions (good), with two tweaks and two additions:
- **Tweak Q2 `why`**: append "...*because the matrix is diagonal* (more generally, triangular). For a general matrix the diagonal entries are NOT the eigenvalues."
- **New Q (existence / complex eigenvalues)**: "What are the eigenvectors of a 90° rotation matrix `[[0,−1],[1,0]]`?" Options: (a) `[1,0]` and `[0,1]`; (b) `[1,1]` and `[1,−1]`; (c) **It has no real eigenvectors — a rotation knocks every vector off its span (eigenvalues are ±i)**; (d) Every vector is an eigenvector. Targets the lesson's biggest over-generalization. Self-contained (matrix given inline).
- **New Q (eigenvalue meaning / λ=0)**: "If a matrix M has an eigenvalue λ = 0, what must be true?" Options: (a) M is the identity; (b) **M is non-invertible (det = 0) — it crushes that eigendirection to zero**; (c) M is a rotation; (d) M has no other eigenvalues. Ties eigen ↔ determinant ↔ invertibility.
- **Optional New Q (diagonalization / powers)**: "Why is it easy to compute M^100 once you have M's eigenvectors and eigenvalues?" Answer: "In the eigenbasis M is diagonal, so M^100 just raises each eigenvalue to the 100th power." Targets the diagonalization payoff. Self-contained.
- **Optional New Q (symmetric ⇒ orthogonal)**: "A covariance matrix is symmetric. What does that guarantee about its eigenvectors?" Answer: "They can be chosen orthogonal (and the eigenvalues are real) — the spectral theorem — which is why PCA's axes are perpendicular." Self-contained.
