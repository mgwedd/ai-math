## Dig Deeper appendix (curated — graduate-authoritative)

### Read
- [Exponentiation (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation) — The canonical reference for all four rules (product, quotient, power-of-power, fractional), the continuity from integer to rational to real exponents via a^x = e^(x ln a), and the critical caveat that the rule (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ breaks for negative bases with non-integer exponents. The lesson teaches the product rule but omits quotient and power-of-power rules — both are here.
- [MIT 18.01 Single Variable Calculus, Lecture 6: Exponential and Log (OCW)](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-01-single-variable-calculus-fall-2006/resources/lecture-6-exponential-and-log/) — Prof. David Jerison's canonical treatment of exponential and logarithmic functions, their derivatives (d/dx eˣ = eˣ, d/dx ln x = 1/x), and the a^x = e^(x ln a) identity. Grounds the fractional-power and power-of-power rules at the calculus level; the authoritative next step after this lesson's deeper card on roots as fractional powers. (MIT 18.01, canonical course.)
- [Vanishing Gradient Problem (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanishing_gradient_problem) — Graduate-level explanation of how repeated multiplication of Jacobian norms (not a single scalar) produces exponential decay or growth in depth. Corrects the lesson's "0.5¹⁰ ≈ 0.001" scalar caricature and provides the real mechanism: the product of per-layer Jacobian spectral norms compounds exponentially with depth.
- [Sebastian Raschka: Accelerating LLMs with Mixed-Precision Techniques](https://sebastianraschka.com/blog/2023/llm-mixed-precision-copy.html) — Concrete fp16/bf16/fp32 dynamic-range table (fp16 max ≈ 65504, min normal ≈ 6×10⁻⁵; fp32 max ≈ 3.4×10³⁸; bfloat16 keeps fp32 range at 7 mantissa bits). Grounds the lesson's vague "cannot hold 10⁻⁴⁰⁰" claim with numbers practitioners actually encounter.

## Science & depth recommendations (to reach master's level)

- **Missing quotient and power-of-power rules** → The lesson teaches the product rule (aᵐ·aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ) but omits aᵐ/aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ and (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ. The latter is the exact trap the quiz's "10³·10⁴" item relies on learners not committing — yet it's never taught, only negatively implied. Adding both rules side by side forces the add-vs-multiply contrast to be learned, not guessed. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation).
- **Vanishing-gradient mechanism is a scalar caricature** → The ml note's "0.5¹⁰ ≈ 0.001" is technically imprecise (0.5¹⁰ = 1/1024 ≈ 0.000977) and mechanistically wrong: gradients vanish/explode because backprop multiplies one Jacobian per layer; the product of their norms compounds exponentially in depth. Label the 0.5¹⁰ example as a scalar illustration and point to the real mechanism. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Vanishing Gradient Problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanishing_gradient_problem) and [arXiv 1801.03744](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1801.03744).
- **Float format dynamic ranges should be concrete** → The lesson says floating point "cannot hold 10⁻⁴⁰⁰" without giving the actual limits. Replace with: fp32 dies below ~10⁻³⁸ and above ~3.4×10³⁸; fp16 is far narrower (max ≈ 65504, min normal ≈ 6×10⁻⁵), which is the concrete reason bf16 and loss-scaling exist. Grounded in [Raschka 2023](https://sebastianraschka.com/blog/2023/llm-mixed-precision-copy.html) and [Kalamkar et al. arXiv 1905.12322](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1905.12322).
- **Negative-base caveat for fractional exponents** → The deeper card on roots as fractional powers never warns that (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ breaks for negative bases: ((−1)²)^(1/2) = 1 but (−1)^(2·½) = −1. The rigorous definition for real exponents is a^x = e^(x ln a), requiring a > 0. One clause prevents a durable misconception. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation).
- **Plant the log bridge** → The lesson correctly identifies that "multiply ⇒ add exponents" is the same fact as "logs turn multiplication into addition" but never connects them explicitly. A one-sentence pointer at the end of `learn` ("Notice: a log is an exponent — the product rule already contains the next big idea") seeds the following lesson and rewards re-reading.
