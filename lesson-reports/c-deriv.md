# c-deriv — The Derivative: Slope, Live

## Current summary (what it teaches + what the lab does)
The lesson introduces the derivative as the limit of secant slopes. It presents the secant formula `slope = (f(a+h) − f(a))/h`, then takes `h → 0` to get the tangent slope `f′(a) = lim_{h→0} (f(a+h) − f(a))/h`. It uses `f(x) = x²/2` as the running example (`f′(x) = x`) and frames the derivative as a *new function* reporting slope everywhere. The `ml` note connects this to gradients of the loss w.r.t. each weight ("70B slopes per step"). The quiz has 3 questions (definition of f′(a); slope of x²/2 at x=3; meaning of f′(c)=0). WRONG_WHY (`c-deriv` block in index.js ~line 1127) gives per-distractor explanations. There is **no `deeper` field** for this lesson.

Lab (`INTERACTIVES.deriv`, index.js ~line 578): plots `f(x)=x²/2`. A draggable blue point sets `a`; a slider sets `h` (0.01–2). A gold **secant** line is drawn through `(a, f(a))` and `(a+h, f(a+h))`; a dashed green **true tangent** (slope `a`) is drawn for comparison. Readout shows `a`, `h`, secant slope, and true `f′(a)`. Three missions: (1) shrink `h ≤ 0.05` to watch secant→tangent; (2) with `h ≤ 0.1`, park where slope ≈ 2; (3) find the flat spot, slope ≈ 0.

## Strengths
- The secant→tangent merge with a **ghost true-tangent overlay** is exactly the right visualization — it makes the limit visceral and lets the learner *see* the approximation error shrink. This matches how 3Blue1Brown and standard calculus courses teach it.
- Using `f(x)=x²/2` (so `f′(x)=x`) is a clean pedagogical choice: the derivative is the identity line, easy to verify mentally.
- Mission 3 (find the flat spot) plants the optimization seed (f′=0 ↔ minima/maxima) early, which pays off in the later c-optim lesson.
- The "derivative is a new function" framing is correct and important.
- The forward-difference formula used in the lab is genuinely the definition's quotient — the lab is mathematically honest about *what* it computes.

## Inaccuracies / fidelity issues (each: the issue -> the correct statement -> source URL)
- **"Instantaneous rate of change" stated without nuance.** The `learn` and quiz answer call f′(a) "the instantaneous rate of change" as if unproblematic. -> This phrase is, strictly, a contradiction: change requires two instants; a single instant has "no more room for change." The honest framing (which a master's-bound learner should meet) is that the derivative is *the best constant approximation to the rate of change near a point* — the limit of average rates, not a rate measured at one instant. The lesson should at least acknowledge the paradox rather than assert the slogan. -> https://www.3blue1brown.com/lessons/derivatives/
- **Silent assumption that the limit always exists.** The lesson only ever shows a smooth curve and never states the precondition. -> The derivative exists only when the **two-sided** limit of the difference quotient exists; many continuous functions fail this (corners, cusps, vertical tangents, discontinuities). Differentiability ⇒ continuity, but **not** the converse (`|x|` at 0 is the canonical counterexample). This is a genuine fidelity gap, not nitpicking — it is core to understanding why ReLU's derivative is undefined at 0 and why subgradients exist. -> https://calcworkshop.com/derivatives/continuity-and-differentiability/
- **"Zoom in far enough and every smooth curve becomes a straight line" (subtitle) overclaims.** -> It is true for *differentiable* curves (local linearity is essentially the definition of differentiability) but false at corners/cusps/vertical tangents, which never straighten no matter how far you zoom. The word "smooth" partly saves it, but a beginner will read "smooth" as "looks nice," not "differentiable." Worth a one-line caveat. -> https://scienceinsights.org/where-a-function-is-not-differentiable-corners-to-cusps/
- **The lab's "true tangent" is hard-coded, not measured.** The green line uses slope `= a` (the analytic answer), so the learner sees the secant approach a line that was *told* the answer. This is fine pedagogically but slightly circular — there is no place where the learner discovers `f′(x)=x` from the data; it is asserted in prose. (Not a math error, a learning-design gap — see Lab ideas.)

## Conceptual gaps (what a serious learner still needs)
- **Differentiability vs. continuity.** The single most important missing concept. A learner heading to ML must know: continuous ≠ differentiable; one-sided derivatives; where derivatives fail (corner = unequal one-sided slopes, e.g. |x|; cusp = one-sided slopes → ±∞, e.g. x^(2/3); vertical tangent = both → +∞, e.g. x^(1/3); jump/removable discontinuity = no derivative). This directly motivates ReLU non-differentiability and subgradients downstream. (src: calcworkshop, scienceinsights, tutorchase below)
- **Linear (first-order) approximation framing.** `f(a+h) ≈ f(a) + f′(a)·h`. This is the *payoff* of the derivative and the conceptual bridge to gradient descent, Taylor series, and Newton's method. The lesson shows the tangent line but never names it as "the best linear approximation near a," which is the framing that generalizes to gradients and Jacobians.
- **Notation literacy.** Leibniz `df/dx`, Lagrange `f′`, and operator `d/dx` are used interchangeably across the curriculum but never reconciled. The `dh→0` idea and why `df/dx` is *not* a fraction (but behaves like one via the chain rule) deserves a sentence.
- **One-sided limits / left- and right-hand derivatives** — needed to make "the limit must exist" concrete.
- **Numerical reality of finite `h`.** The lab *is* a forward-difference (`O(h)` accurate) computation. A master's learner should know: forward difference error is `O(h)`; **central difference** `(f(a+h) − f(a−h))/(2h)` is `O(h²)` (far better for the same h); and crucially, making h *too small* blows up due to floating-point **subtractive cancellation** (round-off error ∝ ε_machine / h). The "shrink h toward 0" mission, taken literally in float, eventually gets *worse*, not better — a beautiful, ML-relevant surprise. -> https://fiveable.me/numerical-analysis-i/unit-10/error-analysis-numerical-differentiation/study-guide/tE7epodXL71V6F45
- **Connection from "slope" to "gradient."** The ml note jumps to 70B weights but never says the 1-variable derivative generalizes to a *partial* derivative per weight, assembled into the gradient vector. A one-line forward pointer would help.

## Lab ideas (concrete: name the interactive, what the learner manipulates, what it reveals)
- **`deriv` upgrade — "Discover f′ by tracing":** Add a faint trail that plots the *measured* secant slope at the current `a` as a point `(a, slope)`. As the learner drags across the domain (with small h), they paint out the cyan derivative curve themselves and *discover* it is the line `y = x` — instead of being told. (The sibling `rules` lab already plots f′ as a curve; bring that discovery here.) New mission: "Drag across the curve and trace out the slope graph — what shape is it?"
- **`deriv` upgrade — secant error readout:** Show `|secant slope − true slope|` numerically and let the learner watch it roughly **halve when h halves** (the O(h) signature). This turns the limit into a measured rate of convergence.
- **New lab `deriv-cancel` — "When zooming in backfires":** Plot the *error* of the forward difference vs. `h` on a log–log axis as the learner shrinks `h` from 1 down past 1e-12. The error falls (truncation, slope −1) then rises (round-off, slope +1), with a V-shaped minimum near `h ≈ √ε ≈ 1e-8`. Mission: "Find the h that gives the *most* accurate slope — is smaller always better?" Reveals subtractive cancellation viscerally. Also introduce central difference as a toggle and watch the trough deepen.
- **New lab `deriv-corner` — "Where the slope breaks":** A chip selector for `f`: `x²/2` (smooth), `|x|` (corner), `x^(1/3)` (vertical tangent), `x^(2/3)` (cusp). Learner drags the point toward x=0 and watches the left/right secant slopes; readout shows left-slope and right-slope separately. Mission: "Find the function where the slope from the left and the slope from the right disagree — that's where the derivative doesn't exist." Plants differentiability vs continuity.

## Content improvements (specific learn/ml/deeper text upgrades)
- **Add the paradox honestly.** Replace the bare "instantaneous rate of change" with one sentence: *"'Instantaneous rate of change' is a slight abuse — a single instant has no change in it. Precisely, f′(a) is the best constant rate that approximates f near a: the limit the average rates close in on."* (3Blue1Brown framing.)
- **Name the tangent as the best linear approximation.** Add: *"Rearranged, this says `f(a+h) ≈ f(a) + f′(a)·h` for small h — the tangent line is the best straight-line stand-in for f near a. Every gradient step, every Taylor expansion, every Newton iteration is built on this one idea."*
- **State the existence condition.** Add: *"The limit has to exist — and from both sides. At a corner like |x|, the slope from the left (−1) and right (+1) disagree, so there's no derivative at 0. Differentiable curves are exactly the ones that look straight when you zoom in; corners and cusps never do."*
- **Add a `deeper` field** (this lesson currently has none, unlike its siblings). Suggested cards:
  - *Differentiability vs continuity:* smooth ⇒ continuous, but continuous ⇏ smooth; the four failure modes (corner, cusp, vertical tangent, jump) with one-line examples (|x|, x^(2/3), x^(1/3), step).
  - *Forward vs central difference:* forward is O(h); central `(f(a+h)−f(a−h))/(2h)` is O(h²); and why h *too* small hurts (round-off / cancellation) — autograd avoids this entirely by using exact rules, not finite differences (forward pointer to c-rules / c-chain).
  - *Notation:* `f′`, `df/dx`, `d/dx` are the same operator; `df/dx` is suggestive of rise/run but is a *limit*, not a literal fraction.
- **Upgrade the `ml` note.** Add the missing bridge: *"In ≥1 variable, the slope-per-weight is a **partial derivative**; stack them into a vector and you have the **gradient**. Note autograd does NOT use the lab's finite-difference quotient — that suffers round-off and costs one forward pass per weight; it uses exact derivative rules (next lessons)."* This corrects an implicit suggestion that training computes slopes the way the lab does.

## Quiz improvements (specific misconceptions to target; keep questions self-contained — never require recalling lab-graph data)
- Q2 currently says "You verified this pattern by shrinking h in the lab" in the WRONG_WHY — fine as flavor, but the question itself is self-contained (good; keep). 
- **Add a differentiability question:** *"For f(x) = |x|, what is f′(0)?"* Options: 0 / 1 / −1 / it doesn't exist (the slope from the left is −1 and from the right is +1, so the two-sided limit fails). Targets the continuous-but-not-differentiable misconception. Self-contained.
- **Add a linear-approximation question:** *"Near a, the tangent line gives the best estimate `f(a+h) ≈ ___`."* Options including `f(a) + f′(a)·h` (correct), `f(a)·f′(a)`, `f′(a)·h`, `f(a) + f(h)`. Reinforces the framing that matters for gradient descent.
- **Add a finite-h / numerical question** (master's depth): *"To estimate a slope numerically, you shrink h. Why can making h extremely tiny make the answer **worse**?"* Options: floating-point round-off / subtractive cancellation dominates (correct); the limit stops existing; the function changes; computers can't divide. Targets the real misconception that "smaller h is always better."
- **Sharpen the definition distractors:** the current "instantaneous rate of change" answer is right but, given the paradox above, consider WRONG_WHY text that distinguishes *average* rate (needs two points) from the derivative as the *limit* of those averages — reinforcing why one-point "rate" is subtle.
