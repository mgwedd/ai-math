# pre-powers — Powers, Roots & Scale

## Current summary (what it teaches + what the lab does)
The lesson (`/Users/wedd/dev/ai-math-worktrees/mystifying-clarke-7ceb80/lib/curriculum/extra.js`, lines 684–799) introduces exponents as repeated multiplication and the four operational rules a learner reuses forever: product rule (aᵐ·aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ), zero power (a⁰ = 1), negative power (a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ), and fractional power as root (a^(1/2) = √a). It then pivots to **scale literacy**: scientific notation, orders of magnitude, and the claim that ML spans the whole range in one breath (lr 10⁻³, ε 10⁻⁸, 10⁹ params). The `ml` note ties this to vanishing/exploding gradients and log-space. Three `deeper` cards cover (1) the downward-halving pattern that forces a⁰=1, (2) orders of magnitude as a log scale, (3) roots as fractional powers feeding the power rule for differentiation.

Two labs:
- **`powrules`** (lines 726–758): a base-selector (2/3/10) plus an exponent slider n ∈ [−3, 6]. Plots y = baseˣ on a Cartesian plane, drops a dot at (n, baseⁿ), and shows a symbolic expansion (`2³ = 2×2×2`, `2⁻¹ = 1/2`, `2^0.5 = √2`). Missions: hit 2ⁿ=8, use a negative exponent to get value < 1, set n = 0.5.
- **`sci`** (lines 761–799): a **logarithmic ruler** from 10⁻⁹ to 10⁹ with a draggable marker (integer exponents only). Gold labels anchor real ML quantities (Adam ε = 1e-8, lr ≈ 1e-3, dropout 0.1, 1M/1B params). Missions: slide to 1e-3, 1e9, 1e-8.

## Strengths
- The "walk the pattern downward" justification for a⁰=1 and negative exponents (deeper card 1) is exactly the pedagogically correct framing — it shows the rules are *forced* by consistency, not conventions. This matches Wikipedia's "definition preserves the product rule" rationale.
- The `sci` log-ruler is genuinely well-designed: anchoring abstract powers of ten to *concrete ML quantities the learner will actually meet* (ε, lr, param counts) is the single best way to build scale intuition, and it correctly makes each tick a 10× jump.
- The quiz traps are well-chosen and the WRONG_WHY explanations name the specific misconception per distractor (e.g. "−6 = 2·(−3): that's multiplication, not exponentiation"). This is high-quality.
- Linking fractional exponents forward to d/dx xⁿ = n·xⁿ⁻¹ with n=½ (deeper card 3) is a strong cross-world thread.
- The `powrules` symbolic expansion (showing `2³ = 2×2×2`) directly visualizes "repeated multiplication," reinforcing the core definition rather than just plotting a curve.

## Inaccuracies / fidelity issues (each: the issue -> the correct statement -> source URL)
1. **"0.5¹⁰ ≈ 0.001" in the `ml` note is wrong by ~2×.** 0.5¹⁰ = 1/1024 ≈ 0.000977 ≈ **0.001 only if you round generously**, which is borderline acceptable — BUT the bigger fidelity problem is the *mechanism* it implies. Vanishing gradients are not primarily "a fixed factor < 1 raised to the depth." They arise from **repeated multiplication of layer Jacobians**, and the relevant quantity is the product of Jacobian spectral norms / singular values, not a single scalar like 0.5. -> Correct statement: gradients vanish or explode because backprop multiplies one Jacobian per layer; if the per-layer factor (the Jacobian's norm / largest singular value) is consistently below 1 the product decays exponentially in depth, above 1 it grows exponentially. The 0.5¹⁰ example is a *scalar caricature* and should be labeled as such. -> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanishing_gradient_problem and https://arxiv.org/pdf/1801.03744
2. **"Floating-point numbers physically cannot hold 10⁻⁴⁰⁰" is true but the lesson never gives the actual limits, leaving a false impression that the boundary is vague.** -> Correct, concrete statement: IEEE-754 **float32** ranges to ≈ 3.4×10³⁸ (max) with smallest normal ≈ 1.2×10⁻³⁸ (subnormals reach ≈ 1.4×10⁻⁴⁵); **float16** caps at 65504 with smallest normal ≈ 6.1×10⁻⁵; **bfloat16** keeps float32's ≈10³⁸ range but with only 7 mantissa bits. So 10⁻⁴⁰⁰ underflows to exactly 0.0 in every common format, and 10⁻⁸ × 10⁻⁸ = 10⁻¹⁶ is already fine in float32 but underflows badly in fp16. -> https://sebastianraschka.com/blog/2023/llm-mixed-precision-copy.html and https://arxiv.org/pdf/1905.12322
3. **The fractional-exponent rule is stated only for the benign case (a^(1/2)=√a) with no caveat about negative bases.** A master's-bound learner should know the identity (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ *breaks* for non-integer exponents on negative bases: ((−1)²)^(1/2) = 1 but (−1)^(2·1/2) = −1. -> Correct statement: rational-exponent rules hold cleanly for **positive** real bases; for real exponents the rigorous definition is a^x = e^(x ln a), which requires a > 0. -> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation
4. **No statement of the quotient or power-of-power rules.** The lesson gives the product rule but omits aᵐ/aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ and (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ. The latter is precisely the rule the *correct* quiz answer to "10³·10⁴" relies on the learner NOT confusing with the product rule — yet it's never taught, so the "10¹² is the trap" framing teaches by negative example only. -> Add both rules explicitly. -> https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation

## Conceptual gaps (what a serious learner still needs)
- **Power rule vs. power-of-power rule contrast.** The single most common exponent error in ML code (and the lesson's own headline quiz trap) is aᵐ·aⁿ vs (aᵐ)ⁿ. The lesson should present both side by side so the learner *derives* why one adds and one multiplies, rather than memorizing "don't multiply."
- **The bridge to logs is asserted, not shown.** The `ml` note says we "work in log-space" but the lesson never connects "multiply ⇒ add exponents" to "logs turn × into +" — that's the same fact viewed twice and is the natural hook into `pre-logs`. A learner who sees log(a·b)=log a + log b is the *exponent rule read backwards* gains a durable mental model.
- **Concrete float-format dynamic range** (see inaccuracy 2). Knowing fp16 overflows at 65504 and underflows at ~6e-5 is the practical reason loss-scaling and bf16 exist — directly relevant to anyone training models.
- **log-sum-exp / the max-subtraction trick.** The `ml` note motivates log-space but never shows the canonical stabilization: softmax/cross-entropy subtract the max before exp so every exponential lands in (0,1], avoiding overflow. This is the most-used numerical-stability idiom in ML and is a perfect, concrete payoff for "powers explode." -> https://academic.oup.com/imajna/article/41/4/2311/5893596
- **Why exponential growth dominates polynomial.** "Each +1 multiplies" is stated, but the learner doesn't see that this is qualitatively different from "each +1 adds a fixed amount" (linear) — the defining contrast that makes compounding, learning-rate decay schedules, and gradient explosion intuitive.

## Lab ideas (concrete: name the interactive, what the learner manipulates, what it reveals)
1. **`gradchain` — Jacobian product vs depth (fixes inaccuracy 1).** A row of N "layers" (slider: N = 1…30) each multiplying the signal by a factor r (slider r ∈ [0.5, 2.0]). Plot the running product rᴺ on a log y-axis. Missions: (a) find an r where the signal survives 20 layers (r≈1); (b) make it vanish below 1e-6; (c) make it explode above 1e6. Readout shows rᴺ in scientific notation and labels "vanishing / stable / exploding." This turns the abstract `ml` claim into the lesson's headline interactive and teaches the *real* mechanism (repeated multiplication compounding exponentially in depth).
2. **`fpfloor` — floating-point underflow ruler (extends `sci`).** Add three colored bands to the existing `sci` ruler marking the representable range of fp16, bf16, fp32. As the learner slides, the readout flips to "OVERFLOW (→ ∞)" or "UNDERFLOW (→ 0.0)" when the marker crosses a format's boundary. Reveals *why* the same number is fine in fp32 but dies in fp16 — the concrete payoff for inaccuracy 2.
3. **`logbridge` — multiply-as-add (preview of `pre-logs`).** Two exponent sliders m and n for a fixed base. Show aᵐ · aⁿ assembled as a stack of (m+n) base-blocks, with the live equation aᵐ·aⁿ = a^(m+n). A toggle reveals the same operation as logs: log(aᵐ·aⁿ) = m+n. This visually unifies the product rule with the log identity, seeding World 2.
4. **`powrules` upgrade — add a log-scale y-axis toggle.** The current plot clips at y=14, so n=6 with base 10 (= 1,000,000) is invisible. A "log y" toggle would let the learner *see* that baseˣ becomes a straight line on a log axis — the single most important visual fact connecting exponentials and log scales, and it reuses the existing `sci` insight.

## Content improvements (specific learn/ml/deeper text upgrades)
- **Add the quotient and power-of-power rules** to the `learn` bullet list: "Divide ⇒ subtract exponents (aᵐ/aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ)" and "Power of a power ⇒ multiply exponents ((aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ)." Put the latter directly next to the product rule so the add-vs-multiply contrast is explicit, not just a quiz gotcha.
- **Fix the `ml` note's vanishing-gradient mechanism.** Replace "Gradients vanish (0.5¹⁰ ≈ 0.001)" with something like: "Backprop multiplies one factor per layer. If each is ~0.5, ten layers give 0.5¹⁰ ≈ 1/1000 — a *scalar caricature* of the real effect, where the product of layer Jacobian norms decays (vanish) or grows (explode) exponentially in depth." Note 0.5¹⁰ = 1/1024.
- **Make the float limits concrete in `ml`.** Replace the vague "cannot hold 10⁻⁴⁰⁰" with the actual numbers: float32 dies below ~10⁻³⁸ and above ~3.4×10³⁸; fp16 is far narrower (65504 down to ~6×10⁻⁵), which is the whole reason bf16 and loss-scaling exist.
- **Add a `deeper` card on log-sum-exp / max-subtraction** as the concrete payoff: "Softmax computes eˣ, which overflows for large x. The fix: subtract the max first, so every exponent lands in (0,1] — the most common numerical-stability trick in ML, and a direct consequence of the exponent rules."
- **Add a one-line caveat** in deeper card 3: rational-exponent rules assume a positive base; for real exponents the rigorous meaning is a^x = e^(x ln a) (a>0).
- **Plant the log bridge** at the end of `learn`: "Notice 'multiply ⇒ add exponents' already contains the next big idea — logs turn multiplication into addition because a log *is* an exponent (World 2)."

## Quiz improvements (specific misconceptions to target; keep questions self-contained)
- **Add a power-of-power item:** "Simplify (10³)⁴." Correct: 10¹². Distractors: 10⁷ (wrongly applies the product rule — the exact mirror of the existing 10³·10⁴ trap, forcing the learner to distinguish the two), 10⁸¹, 30¹². This pairs with the existing question to teach add-vs-multiply directly.
- **Add a quotient/negative item:** "Simplify 10²/10⁵." Correct: 10⁻³. Distractors: 10⁷ (added instead of subtracted), 10³ (subtracted backwards), 5⁻³. Targets the sign-of-the-difference error.

It can represent (a) any number up to ~10³⁸ like float32, (b) numbers only up to ~65,500, (c) 10⁻⁴⁰⁰ exactly, (d) negative exponents but not positive ones." Correct: (b). Teaches the concrete limit without referencing any lab graph.
- **Strengthen the orders-of-magnitude item** with a distractor that targets the ratio/count confusion more sharply, e.g. add "1,000,000,000 (the raw count)" — the existing options are good; this reinforces that "orders apart" = exponent difference, not the value.
