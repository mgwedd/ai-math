## Dig Deeper appendix (curated — graduate-authoritative)

### Read

- [Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis — Introduction to Probability, 2nd ed. (MIT OCW)](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/res-6-012-introduction-to-probability-spring-2018/d973b10c2587781f86ca4f2aff49098f_MITRES_6_012S18_Textbook.pdf) — Free OCW PDF of the canonical MIT probability textbook. Chapter 2 covers the Bernoulli and binomial with exact PMF formulas, precise variance formulas, and the connection Bernoulli = Binomial(n=1,p). Chapter 3 gives the Gaussian density, the 68-95-99.7 rule, and the normal approximation to the binomial with the continuity correction — all at graduate rigor. The authoritative source for the lesson's three distribution families.
- [Bishop — Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning, Ch. 2 (Microsoft Research PDF)](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2006/01/Bishop-Pattern-Recognition-and-Machine-Learning-2006.pdf) — The definitive graduate ML textbook (freely mirrored by the author's employer). Chapter 2 unifies Bernoulli, binomial, Gaussian, multinomial, Dirichlet, Beta, and Poisson under the exponential family framework p(x|η) = h(x)·exp(η(θ)·T(x) − A(θ)) — exactly the "exponential family" deeper card in the lesson. The connection between softmax/sigmoid outputs, sufficient statistics, and GLMs is made precise in §2.4. Peer-reviewed textbook, Springer.
- [Stanford CS109 Lecture Notes: Continuous Distributions](https://web.stanford.edu/class/archive/cs/cs109/cs109.1208/lectureNotes/LN09_continuous_rv.pdf) — Free PDF from Stanford's Probability for Computer Scientists. Covers the Gaussian (normal) distribution with the CDF, standardization, Z-scores, and the normal approximation to the binomial — complementing the lesson's binomial-to-bell visual with the analytical conditions (np ≥ 5, n(1−p) ≥ 5). Well-suited for CS/ML audiences.

## Science & depth recommendations (to reach master's level)

- **The exponential family deeper card is the lesson's best depth hook but stops at the template.** Bishop PRML §2.4 shows *why* this matters: GLMs (logistic regression, Poisson regression) all put an exponential-family distribution on the output and optimize the log-likelihood, giving clean, closed-form gradient updates. Connecting the lesson's template "p(x|η) = h(x)·exp(η(θ)·T(x)−A(θ))" to the gradient of log-likelihood (which is just T(x) − E[T(X)], the sufficient-statistic residual) would turn an interesting fact into an operational insight. Source: Bishop PRML §2.4; Stanford CS229.
- **The normal approximation condition ("np ≥ 5 and n(1−p) ≥ 5") is stated but its failure mode is not shown.** The lesson correctly notes that "with p = 0.02, you can have a large n and still have a lopsided binomial." This should be given a number: for p = 0.02, n = 100, the CLT approximation is poor because np = 2 < 5; the Poisson approximation with λ = np is much better in this regime. Naming the Poisson as the right approximation for rare events (small p, large n, np moderate) would make the lesson's caveat actionable. Source: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis Ch. 2; Wikipedia, Poisson distribution.
- **The categorical/softmax deeper card should connect temperature to the maximum-entropy principle.** The lesson says "temperature scales scores before softmax: low T sharpens, high T flattens" without explaining *why* flat is interesting: a high-temperature softmax approaches the maximum-entropy (uniform) distribution over the vocabulary, which is the "least committal" prior. This connects the lesson's earlier "Gaussian is the maximum-entropy bell" claim to softmax, showing that the same principle (entropy maximization under constraints) governs both. Source: Bishop PRML §2.1; Goodfellow et al. §3.13.
