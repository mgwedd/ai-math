# pre-logs — Logarithms: The Inverse Question

## Dig Deeper appendix (curated — graduate-authoritative)

### Read
- [Logarithm (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm) — The canonical reference for the formal definition, domain restriction (log_b(x) requires x > 0, b > 0, b ≠ 1), all identities (product, quotient, power, change of base), Napier's 1614 invention, and the connection to ln via natural log. Covers the quotient rule the lesson mentions only as a quiz distractor, and explains why log x → −∞ as x → 0⁺ — the critical fact behind exploding cross-entropy loss.
- [3Blue1Brown: Logarithm Fundamentals lesson notes](https://www.3blue1brown.com/lessons/ldm-logarithms/) — Grant Sanderson's companion text to the Lockdown Math Ep. 6 video, covering the inverse relationship, the "how many times do I divide?" mnemonic, the product/power rules, and the log scale in ML. The most insightful single-page treatment for the target audience; bridges directly to the lesson's loginv and logmul labs.
- [Log-Sum-Exp Trick (Gregory Gundersen)](https://gregorygundersen.com/blog/2020/02/09/log-sum-exp/) — Peer-quality technical blog: shows that summing log-probabilities fixes the product underflow, but computing log(softmax) still overflows unless you subtract the max first (the LSE trick). The lesson presents the log as a complete numerical fix; this adds the essential caveat and the canonical idiom frameworks expose as `log_softmax` and `logsumexp`.
- [Cross-Entropy (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-entropy) — Formal definition H(p,q) = −Σ p(x) log q(x) as a *weighted* sum, distinguished from negative log-likelihood. Fixes the lesson's loose "−Σ log p" shorthand and provides the one-hot special case that makes both descriptions compatible.

### Watch
- [3Blue1Brown: Logarithm Fundamentals | Lockdown Math Ep. 6](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cEvgcoyZvB4) (3Blue1Brown, ~92 min) — The definitive visual treatment: inverse relationship, the multiplication-to-addition identity shown spatially (slide-rule style), change of base, and log-scale intuition with ML applications. The logmul lab in the lesson is essentially a compact version of this video's central demonstration.
- [3Blue1Brown: What makes the natural log "natural"? | Lockdown Math Ep. 7](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4PDoT7jtxmw) (3Blue1Brown, ~75 min) — Explains why e is the natural base: d/dx eˣ = eˣ and d/dx ln x = 1/x give the cleanest derivatives, which is precisely why ML defaults to ln — log-likelihood and cross-entropy gradients simplify to beautiful forms only with natural log. Directly fills the lesson's biggest conceptual gap (why ln specifically).
- [MIT 18.01 Lecture 6: Exponential and Log](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-01-single-variable-calculus-fall-2006/resources/lecture-6-exponential-and-log/) (MIT OCW, Prof. David Jerison) — Rigorous single-variable calculus treatment: d/dx ln x = 1/x derived, logarithmic differentiation, and the a^x = e^(x ln a) bridge. The authoritative next step for learners moving from "log is an inverse" to "log has a clean derivative."

## Science & depth recommendations (to reach master's level)

- **Missing domain restriction** → The lesson never states log_b(x) requires x > 0, b > 0, b ≠ 1, or that log x → −∞ as x → 0⁺. This is not pedantry: a confidently-wrong classifier drives its predicted probability toward 0, sending −log q → +∞ — the gradient signal that drives learning. Add one sentence in `learn` on domain and the behavior near 0. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm).
- **Missing quotient rule** → Product and power rules are taught; log(a/b) = log a − log b appears only as a quiz distractor. The quotient rule is essential for KL divergence (Σ p log(p/q)), logits = log(p/(1−p)), and any log-ratio. Add it beside the product rule. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm).
- **Why ln specifically** → The change-of-base card correctly says all logs differ by a constant but never says why ML defaults to ln: d/dx ln x = 1/x (the cleanest derivative), so log-likelihood and cross-entropy gradients simplify to closed forms only with natural log. Add this as one clause in the change-of-base card. Grounded in [MIT 18.01 Lecture 6](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-01-single-variable-calculus-fall-2006/resources/lecture-6-exponential-and-log/) and [3Blue1Brown Ep. 7](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4PDoT7jtxmw).
- **Cross-entropy is weighted, not just a sum of logs** → The ml note's "−Σ log p" is the negative log-likelihood for one-hot labels. True cross-entropy is H(p,q) = −Σ p(x) log q(x) — an expectation under the true distribution. Fix with the full formula and note the one-hot special case that makes both compatible. Also fix perplexity: it is the exponentiated *cross-entropy* (effective branching factor), not exponentiated entropy of a single distribution. Grounded in [Wikipedia: Cross-Entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-entropy) and [Wikipedia: Perplexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perplexity).
- **Log-sum-exp caveat** → The lesson presents "take log of the product → safe sum" as a complete numerical fix, but log(softmax(x)) overflows/underflows for large |x| unless you subtract the max first: LSE = a + log Σ e^{xᵢ−a}. Add a forward-pointer so learners don't reach for log(softmax(...)) naively. Grounded in [Gundersen: Log-Sum-Exp](https://gregorygundersen.com/blog/2020/02/09/log-sum-exp/).
