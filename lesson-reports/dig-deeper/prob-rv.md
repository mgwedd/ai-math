# prob-rv — Random Variables & Expectation

## Note: an existing audit report (`lesson-reports/prob-rv.md`) covers inaccuracies, gaps, and lab ideas in detail. This dig-deeper file adds curated graduate-authoritative sources and videos not present in that report.

## Dig Deeper appendix (curated — graduate-authoritative)

### Read

- [Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis — Introduction to Probability, 2nd ed. (MIT OCW)](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/res-6-012-introduction-to-probability-spring-2018/d973b10c2587781f86ca4f2aff49098f_MITRES_6_012S18_Textbook.pdf) — The canonical MIT probability textbook, freely distributed via OCW. Chapter 2 defines random variables as functions from the sample space Ω to ℝ (the measure-theoretic view the lesson glosses over), gives the discrete expectation formula with a careful statement of when it converges, and covers the Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS: E[g(X)] = Σ g(xᵢ)P(X=xᵢ)) and linearity of expectation including the dependence-blind case. The lesson's four quiz questions are all special cases of exercises in Chapter 2.
- [Blitzstein & Hwang — Introduction to Probability, 2nd ed., Ch. 3–4](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1VmkAAGOYCTORq1wxSQqy255qLJjTNvBI/) — Harvard Stat 110 companion text (official free PDF via probabilitybook.net). Chapter 3 covers expectation with Blitzstein's signature care: LOTUS is given a separate section, linearity of expectation is proved both by definition and by the indicator trick, and Jensen's inequality is stated and proved. Chapter 4 (Continuous RVs) gives the integral analogue E[X] = ∫ x f(x) dx — the form a master's student will encounter in every deep learning paper. CRC Press peer-reviewed.
- [Stanford CS229 Probability Review (Kolter & Do)](https://cs229.stanford.edu/section/cs229-prob.pdf) — Free PDF from Stanford's ML course. A concise, formula-dense review at exactly the right level for ML practitioners: covers expectation, variance, covariance, moment generating functions, and the most important properties (linearity, LOTUS, Jensen's). The recommended "prerequisite brushup" for any ML graduate student who took probability years ago. Confirmed live.

### Watch

- [Stat 110 Lecture 9: Expectation, Indicator RVs, Linearity (Blitzstein, stat110harvard)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LX2q356N2rU) — Harvard Stat 110 channel. Blitzstein's lecture on expectation is the best 50-minute treatment available: he proves E[X+Y] = E[X]+E[Y] for *dependent* X, Y using the definition; derives LOTUS; and works through multiple indicator variable problems that show linearity of expectation as a computational tool, not just a formula. Directly addresses the three gaps the lesson leaves: continuous RVs, linearity for dependent variables, and LOTUS.
- [MIT RES.6-012: "Expected Value of a Random Variable" segment (Tsitsiklis, ~10 min)](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLUl4u3cNGP60hI9ATjSFgLZpbNJ7myAg6) — MIT Spring 2018 Introduction to Probability playlist. The segment L06.4 "Expected Value of a Discrete Random Variable" and L06.5 "Expected Value by the Rule of the Lazy Statistician" cover LOTUS formally from scratch. Tsitsiklis's derivation is more algebraic and proof-oriented than Blitzstein's; together they give learners two styles of argument.

## Science & depth recommendations (to reach master's level)

- **The lesson's RV definition ("a number assigned to a random outcome") conflates the random variable with its value.** A random variable is a *function* X: Ω → ℝ from a probability space (Ω, ℱ, P) to the real line; the "randomness" is in selecting ω ∈ Ω according to P, and X(ω) is deterministic. This distinction matters when two RVs share a distribution but are different functions (e.g., X = die face and Y = 7 − X have the same distribution, but X + Y = 7 identically, not randomly). Adding one sentence — "More precisely, a random variable is a function from outcomes to numbers; the randomness is in which outcome occurs, not in the function" — closes this gap. Source: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis Ch. 2; Blitzstein & Hwang Ch. 3.
- **The continuous analogue E[X] = ∫ x f(x) dx is completely absent.** The lesson shows only the discrete formula. Every ML paper involving Gaussians, weight initializations, or continuous reward signals uses the integral. At minimum, a one-line note — "for continuous RVs like Gaussian noise, the sum becomes an integral" — with a pointer to Bertsekas Ch. 3 would prevent the jarring transition students experience when moving to ML textbooks. Source: Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis Ch. 3; Blitzstein & Hwang Ch. 4.
- **LOTUS and Jensen's inequality are in the deeper cards but not in the learn text, leaving a critical gap.** The lesson teaches E[X] but never warns that E[g(X)] ≠ g(E[X]) for nonlinear g. A fair die has E[X] = 3.5 but E[X²] = 91/6 ≈ 15.17 ≠ 12.25. The gap is variance, and Jensen's inequality formalizes the direction: for convex g, E[g(X)] ≥ g(E[X]). Without this, students implicitly assume E passes through functions — the most common expectation error in ML work. Source: Stanford CS229 review; Blitzstein & Hwang Ch. 3.
